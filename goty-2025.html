Even more than previous years, this goty is less of a guide to games and more of an exorcism of gaming thoughts.

2025 has been the year of... uh... alternate means of gaming, and treating most of everything like a permanent Nextfest. Most of it has been trying to find the right, you know, "vacation" games. I'm well-set for "hobby"/"serious" games; I will never run out of good puzzle games and my backlog is only growing, and between those and the occasional big release, I will never stop having a good game for concentration time.

I'm fully set for my "houseplant" game between Warframe and <insert shelves of roguelikes with dailies and idlers here>. Those games are permanently set on my windowsill and getting daily care, and I'll be happy seeing those succulents of games grow. I'm mostly looking for "vacation" games, for my vacation from vacations. Even better if playable by controller while lounging (.........maybe while high). Less game-y games, outright gameless games. Really browsing through games.

Theoretically this means I'm seeing a lot more games and spending more money on games in a more informed way, and that's *mostly* true. Most games filter out fast. There are plenty of kinds of incompatibilities you can sniff out within a quarter hour, maybe one hour of playing a game. Too many of them take a lot longer. I'm even running into a special category of impasse for niche games that look promising but are politically... suspenseful? I think I like what the chefs are cooking but I don't know if I want to support the establishmentWell OK, I do try to buy the game if this phase goes on too long, it's only fair.. "Trying things out" becomes digging myself into a too-deep pit of mysteries upon mysteries.

I guess I'll just have to play those games to completion and figure out what's what?

This scattershot kind of gaming has been great for figuring out the ins and outs of what I actually want from games, but it has been atrocious for building a list of games that have been played to completion.

So, this year, there will be less individual game writeups, and a lot more derailments.

Digression: I don't know what I want from space games

I've been looking for space.

Trying out a shotgun blast of games means diving into some genres without familiarity of the evolution of the genre, and that means my analyses this year will be extra sparsely informed -- but even as a relative stranger, you immediately pick up vibes, evidence, craters. By the time you try your third colony/building sim you can tell that the genre had an extinction-level Slay-the-Spire-type impact event, and now there's a shape to a subgenre that you see all over the place. I can't tell you what exactly happened here, but I can tell you they all involve semi-airtight rooms, control of various gases, temperature simulation, NPCs with stats and emotional states, etc, etc. It's not quite Dwarf Fortress, just one step removed... It's almost certainly RimWorld, right? I think there was a slow wave of games with similar mechanics, but RimWorld was the bottleneck so many other design went through. I think there's a consistent language among space-based peoples manager games now.

...None of those games are making it on to the list though, mostly because these are naturally very large games. Maybe a few years from now. This genre does tend towards being demanding of their players, and puzzle games have near-monopoly on those spoons for me, so I don't have high hopes?

On another corner of unfamiliarity, there's the space traveler genre. By default, my point of reference this year is No Man's Sky. This is not an ideal point of view to work with. Digression: Courier Games

Jumping off Space Trader... There have been genres crystallizing as part of the cozy games movement, and at least from what I've played, courier games are in an interesting spot. It's not a genre that has solidified (there's no template getting copied like Slay the Spire or Vampire Survivors or Symphony of the Night) but there's... a consistent vibe? a direction a several games are sharing. It's the shared endpoint of taking relatively open action-adventure games and then removing all the combat from it. You tend to end up with collection quests and bringing things from one place to another, and the design challenge is making it still feel gamy. It's convergent evolution on trading-based games on the past (Uncharted Waters, early trucking games, Elite), but these aren't quite descendants of those games.

Some of the roots of the genre feel like they came from farming games, but I don't know if they initiate a proper chain of cause and effect all the way to current games. I can sort of argue that farming games have been pushing out in this direction, these games have pushed to be more excursionary. In a way, Stardew Valley and the Runes Factory represent action-adventures coming in to add dungeons and long expeditions. However, these trips don't always revolve around combat, and sometimes you take these trips just for random foraging or revisiting unique shops. And then you have games like My Time at Portia pushing these travels longer and longer, and it seems inevitable that some games will pop out that focus on travel.

So what's been inherited from farming games? You naturally have towns again (sometimes with villagers. and sometimes they even change over time). Day/night clocks almost feel obligatory, but sometimes they don't serve to provide pressure, instead it expresses passage of time and allows the setting to have distinct day- and night-vibes. I might dare to claim that Diaries of a Spaceport JanitorMight still be unmatched in vibe-setting. To this day games only rarely make places quite that populated, that bustling. is one of the earliest games leading into this genre. It's still very gamy because the mission statement is about the pressures of living, and not <the shared mission of explicitly-stated-as-"cozy" games>. Currencies still usually follow over, but that inheritance goes farther back. Infrastructure-as-metroidvania (building bridges, fixing things, etc) often make a return. A lot of the bones are still there.

The missing part of the theory is... I'm having trouble pointing out consistent halfway steps to this genre, at least among games I've played. Instead I have multiple approaches from different directions around 2021? Wytchwood is a mostly peaceful craft'em'up with a lot of gathering and grinding. It's something like fitting the form of Don't Starve to be less about a hostile world, but it still has hostile NPCs and a health system. Garden Story is mostly in the fighty action-adventure pool, but it's also dipping into farming mechanics *and* it's trying to make a multi-town world work. Fixfox feels so close to the genre as it is now, but the details all don't quite fit? I think its open world vibe is sort of off by having the world shuffle itself periodically? It is still very, very ahead of its time though. Can I patch my GOTY list that year to put it higher?

So where are we now? In the main list I've put Starstruck Vagabond, Distant Blooms, and Rekindled Trails. Clearly I can't finish this digression without mentioning Death Stranding (2), but it also can't help being very gamy, so I think its intended audience doesn't necessarily overlap with the other courier games that much? I've also run into Fading Serenades this year, which I'm not as sure about recommending, but you should glance at it. I can't quite put a finger on what it's lacking, but maybe it just needs a little more stuff overall. (Look, this entire GOTY writeup is a crisis about encountering je-ne-sais-quoi-s everywhere, go try some of these games.) I've also played the demo for Letters to Arralla and it looks quite promising, and that one's all about letter deliveryOh! Also shoutouts to Lake.. I wouldn't be surprised if the postal service wrapper will be a common base for this corner of cozy games, but maybe that doesn't happen without some breakout hit annihilating variety going forward.

Rekindled Trails
Courier Adventure

Flip that approach. Distant Bloom spent a lot of effort on its environment and how it changes while leaving the gameplay barebones (and arguably incomplete), while Rekindled Trails here...

Rekindled Trails shares a lot of the same skeleton. These are both largely single-plane walkarounds on an expansive world, there's no jumping (but sometimes you can drop down ledges), and there's even similar jank of sometimes getting stuck in menacing invisible geometry. Instead of cultivation, the central verb of the game is resource harvesting (with mini-minigames) and (indirect?) trade. The world is split into regions of cities and their surroundings, and each region has different resources, each city has a preference for types of goods they don't have, and each city wants particular resources (and money...) to level up. Travel is straightforward but effective -- If you graze past the torches and lamps littered along roads, you get fired up and temporarily move a lot faster. So you run around the world, city to city, gathering resources along the way. The backpack and selling systems are proper inventory tetris with some locational bonuses, like if Moonlighter 1's backpack system evolved further. It's fun!

...But you do a lot of it. It's not at all the intent of the game, but throughout the game I felt the crushing weight of money. This is a problem that eventually solves itself post-game once all the possible item bonuses can play with each other, but while the game paces itself well so that you do minimal grinding to progress the story along, it doesn't give a lot of slack en route. That means when the game regularly introduces new cosmetics to buy, pets to keep, or side upgrades along the way, they feel like they're priced a bit high for that point of the game, and it feels best to skip everything. Maybe the game just needed more things so that not everything feels like they're priced for the postgame. The backpack upgrades are especially harsh; the upgrade price makes economic sense for how valuable things can be in the postgame, but it never feels good to grind for it. Meanwhile, the process of unlocking shortcut routes felt better -- they don't necessarily take less effort, but instead they ask for specific mystery items, and you have to figure out what they are and bring them there. Economically I'm pretty sure the opportunity cost is similar, but they feel like they fit better.

The big spends in the game (and required to propel the plot) are the city upgrades, and here's the other big point of comparison with Bloom. Where Distant Bloom probably spent too much of its effort portraying the world changing, Rekindled Trails felt like it should've done more. The first tier of city upgrades was neat (introducing eggs as slot-fillers is clever!) but there's less... knitting the world together as the story implied? The cities and especially the roads are too static. The shortcuts help a bit, but I'll take Distant Bloom's heavy-handed flowers-everywhere approach over nothing...


That was a long digression on not much wrongness in a random game, but please don't take this as a takedown on the game. Sometimes you play two 7/10 games back to back and you come away with unexpected points of comparison and new things to pay attention to. It's how, once in a long while, the je-ne-sais-quoi gets to congeal into a concrete thought. Sometimes something has to be wrong in the right way.

Digression: Hiking Games
(BeamNG.drive) Right... a driving game...

The talk about gaminess and piracy maybe hinted towards this. I've been spending some time doing something like hiking in games. Just walk and look around. This is an absurd, perverse pit to fall into. By its nature, this is almost the least gaming you can do, but it's often best done with the most expensive games out there with the best rig you can getIt really is VR-less VR... Although! Speaking of! VRChat has plenty of very good worlds to sightsee, and most of them are optimized for the Quest. Those are free and accessible, go try'em out!. It's the complete opposite of weird little puzzle games, and I'm here to tell that it's good. (......spend responsibly)

Rockstar and Rockstar-derived games are naturally very good for this. They've spent so many millions and effort-hours building those worlds, and sometimes the details are even fun to see! And for the most part, the game world lets you do this, you don't have to mod out encounters or anything like that. The game's UI is also relatively hands-off. It's nice to have a world big enough to be able to wander, and then wander without thinking about objectives. Go take a vacation from playing games.

Driving is also hiking, sometimes. The most visible examples are the Truck Simulators (courier games!) and the wide wide genre of transit simulators, but a lot of conventional driving games have gone all-in on open worlds. Since driving games are not at all interested in spicing up travel with gameplay (since the the travel is the gameplay), they are great if you just want to fuck off. I admittedly didn't seek too many racing games out, so I can't give many examples, but they've been great for tourists. The Crew 1Recently resurrected with the The Crew Unlimited project, if you already legally own the game. & 2 have menacingly large expansive worlds for driving, at least when the UI and random challenges aren't being too intrusive. BeamNG.drive isn't quite as expansive as its higher-budget cousins but it's incredible on every other aspect.

I think weird feeling I'm left with in my gut is that budget (both dev-side and customer-side) seems to help a lot. On the dev side, a lot of the very recommendable worlds are AAA+ projects, and in all cases represent many, many, many labor hours. Procedural generation should work (and a lot of survival games kindly let you convert them to wandering games), but something's... It's a je-ne-sais-quoi sort of deal again. Even though random generation theoretically generates infinitely, what you get out of it seems in proportion to the effort you put in, so I don't know if I'd call it labor-saving. On the other side, this kind of gaming can be very low in hours relative to the price if you don't practice the discipline to just... hang around more. Maybe this makes for a good use-case for game streaming services (savor high end graphics! ignore latency issues! dive into vast libraries!), but so much of the streaming concept is feeling more and more off.

I guess in the end these are the same questions that apply to all of gaming, but I worry that this genre is inherently more... disposable? In the design-space there's not a lot of room for walking games to be different from each other since all you have are the world and its looks. Maybe it's because this feels more like the substrate of a game than a game-in-of-itself (and I've mentioned plenty of treating other games as hiking games by ignoring the gamey parts), an anxiety that it would be somehow wasteful if this actually takes off as a genre. Maybe this would be a much more natural genre to exist if world reuse was a much more common thing. Hell, it would be great if there was a genre about appreciating existing maps in other games more. Should I ask for this genre to start existing for real? Walking is so nice, in any case.

The natural follow-up after thinking about dev-side costs would be to talk and muse about GenAI and how it doesn't work even when it functions correctly, but that will be some other article, something more coherent. Maybe 2026 GOTY, if my plans work out.

So. This was a project from a chunk of the community to create a proper introduction to every single piece of the game.
